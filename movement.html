The Movement

Women Who Changed Cannabis

They were never absent from this plant's history. They were its midwives, its healers, its scientists.

Long before cannabis history was written down, women were already shaping it — in kitchens, in clinics, in fields, and in research labs. This is a growing archive of their stories, organized by the kind of work they did. Each profile will eventually have its own dedicated page.

🌿 Activists & Advocates

1922 – 1999

Mary Jane "Brownie Mary" Rathbun

Caregiver, San Francisco

A hospital volunteer who baked thousands of cannabis brownies for AIDS patients during the height of the crisis in the 1980s and 90s. Arrested three times, each arrest drew more public sympathy to the cause. Her advocacy, alongside Dennis Peron, helped pass California's Proposition 215, the first medical cannabis law in the country.

b. 1953

Valerie Corral

Patient Advocate, Santa Cruz

After a car accident left her with a seizure disorder that medication couldn't control, Corral found relief through cannabis and became the first person in California to argue the medical necessity defense in court. In 1993 she co-founded WAMM, one of the nation's first medical cannabis collectives, which survived a dramatic armed DEA raid in 2002 and went on to win a landmark court ruling protecting patients' rights.

b. 1966

Wanda James

Entrepreneur & Navy Veteran, Denver

A former U.S. Navy officer, James and her husband became the first Black couple in the U.S. to legally own a licensed cannabis business when they opened Apothecary of Colorado in 2009, later growing it into Simply Pure. She's since become a national voice for equity and criminal justice reform in the cannabis industry.

🔬 Scientists & Researchers

Active since 1970s

Dr. Melanie Dreher

Nurse & Anthropologist

As a young graduate student, Dreher traveled to rural Jamaica to study cannabis use in pregnancy — one of the first long-term studies of its kind. Published in 1994, her research found no measurable developmental differences between exposed and non-exposed infants. Decades later, the study remains influential and also genuinely debated, a reminder of how much research in this space still needs to happen.

1988

Dr. Allyn Howlett

Neuropharmacologist

Howlett and her graduate student William Devane identified the CB1 cannabinoid receptor in 1988 — the discovery that revealed the body's own endocannabinoid system. It's one of the most important breakthroughs in cannabinoid science, underpinning nearly all modern research into how cannabis affects the body.

2021

Dr. Sue Sisley

Physician & Researcher

After years navigating federal regulatory barriers, Sisley became one of the first researchers in the U.S. to publish results from a federally approved randomized clinical trial on cannabis for PTSD in veterans. The trial proved that rigorous government-sanctioned cannabis research was possible — and pointed to how much more is still needed.

🧈 Makers & Innovators

b. 1944

Mila Jansen

Inventor, Amsterdam

Raising four children alone, Jansen turned an idea inspired by her clothes dryer into the Pollinator — the first mechanical device for separating trichomes from cannabis, unveiled in 1994. She went on to invent the Ice-O-Lator and Bubbleator, foundational tools in modern hash-making still used worldwide. Known as the "Queen of Hash."

⚖️ Policy & Justice

Massachusetts CCC

Shaleen Title

Attorney & Regulator

As one of the founding commissioners of the Massachusetts Cannabis Control Commission, Title helped shape one of the nation's first adult-use cannabis markets built around social equity. She later co-founded the Parabola Center for Law and Policy to continue advocating for fair, restorative cannabis regulation nationally.
